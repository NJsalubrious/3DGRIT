The Problem with Auto-Riggers

▸

Every auto-rigger I've used gives you 80% of what you need and then fights you for the last 20%. That last 20% is where the character lives. The specific shoulder roll behaviour. The exact hip pivot. The way the spine distributes twist across segments. Auto-riggers abstract this away under the assumption that "good enough" is acceptable. It's not.

The 3D_GRIT Bipedal Rig is built from zero. No Rigify. No auto-anything. Every bone is placed with intention, every constraint is authored by hand. This isn't about being difficult. It's about being precise.

Skeleton Hierarchy Design

▸

The hierarchy follows a strict naming convention: L_ / R_ side prefixes, DEF_ for deformation bones, MCH_ for mechanism bones, CTL_ for control bones. No ambiguity. No guesswork when you're scripting on top of the rig.

  • Root → COG → Spine chain (4 segments) — Full FK with optional spline IK overlay
  • Shoulder → Upper Arm → Lower Arm → Hand — IK/FK switching with seamless snapping
  • Hip → Upper Leg → Lower Leg → Foot — Reverse foot lock with ball/toe pivot isolation
  • Twist bones — 2x per upper arm, 2x per upper leg, 1x per forearm

The naming convention isn't cosmetic. It's structural. Every automation script in the 3D_GRIT pipeline reads these prefixes to identify bone roles. Change the naming, you break the pipeline. That's by design — it enforces discipline.

IK/FK Switching Architecture

▸

The switch isn't a single float that blends between two poses. That's the lazy approach and it creates drift. Instead, the IK/FK system uses a snap-on-switch methodology:

  • When switching from FK → IK, the IK target and pole vector are computed from the current FK pose and snapped into position before the influence is toggled
  • When switching from IK → FK, each FK bone in the chain copies the evaluated world-space rotation from the IK result
  • The switch happens in a single frame — no interpolation, no drift, no cleanup

This is handled by a custom operator in the 3D_GRIT panel. The animator presses one button. The math happens behind the scenes. The character doesn't pop. That's the standard.
